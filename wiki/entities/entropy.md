# Entropy

## The Core Concept

Entropy is the measure of disorder or the number of microstates compatible with a macrostate. It is the single quantity that connects thermodynamics, information theory, and quantum mechanics.

## Three Forms of Entropy

**Thermodynamic entropy** (Clausius, 1865): A measure of disorder. The Second Law states entropy of an isolated system never decreases.

**Information-theoretic entropy** (Shannon, 1948): $H = -\sum p_i \log p_i$. Shannon showed that information is just a measure of missing knowledge — the fewer bits needed to specify a state, the higher its entropy.

**Quantum entropy** (von Neumann, 1932): $S = -\text{Tr}(\rho \log \rho)$, where $\rho$ is the density matrix. Pure states have zero entropy; mixed states (decohered) have positive entropy.

The key insight: **information is physical**. The three entropies are the same quantity viewed through different lenses.

## Boltzmann's Equation

Ludwig Boltzmann's equation — $S = k \log W$ — is engraved on his tombstone. It connects entropy to the number of microstates $W$. This is the foundation of statistical mechanics.

## The Arrow of Time

The thermodynamic arrow of time is the most fundamental: time flows forward because entropy increases. But this begs the question — why was entropy so low in the first place?

**The Past Hypothesis** (Albert, 2000): The universe began in an extraordinarily low-entropy state (the Big Bang). This is the deepest unsolved question in cosmology.

## Entropy and Time Travel

Time travel is entropy reconfiguration. If entropy is a property of the field (not absolute), then:
- Time travel = reconfiguring the field so entropy flows "backward"
- UAPs = localized entropy anomalies
- Exotic matter = locally reduces entropy
- AI = information processing that reduces entropy

## Deep Dive: The Many Definitions of Entropy

A 2026 interview with Prof. Wayne Myrvold (University of Western Ontario) explored entropy from multiple angles, revealing deep conceptual disagreements among physicists.

### Clausius vs Boltzmann vs Gibbs

The word "entropy" is used for at least three different quantities:

1. **Clausius entropy** (1865): Defined thermodynamically via $dS = \delta Q_{\text{rev}}/T$. A resource-theoretic quantity: given certain physical resources (heat), how much useful work can you extract?

2. **Boltzmann entropy** ($S = k \log W$): Defined over microstates compatible with a macrostate. Objective — a property of the physical system.

3. **Gibbs entropy** ($S = -k \int \rho \log \rho$): Defined over an ensemble, depends on the observer's knowledge. Subjective — changes when you learn more about the system.

### The Subjectivity Debate

Does entropy decrease when you gain information about a system? Competent physicists give opposite answers:

- **Yes** (information-theoretic view): entropy is about your state of knowledge. Better information = lower entropy.
- **No** (thermodynamic view): entropy is an objective physical property like mass. Your knowledge doesn't change the system.

Myrvold argues the ambiguity arises because people are using different definitions of entropy for different purposes.

### Thermodynamics as Resource Theory

The founders of thermodynamics (Carnot, Clausius, Kelvin) were asking resource-theory questions: given heat at certain temperatures, how much work can you extract? Modern quantum information theory has revived this framing — quantum thermodynamics is a resource theory about what agents with certain resources can achieve.

### The Second Law Is Not What You Think

The common statement "entropy of an isolated system never increases" is actually not Clausius's official statement of the second law. Clausius's definition of entropy **presupposes** the second law — you can only define Clausius entropy for states reachable by reversible processes. The second law is logically prior to Clausius entropy, not the other way around.

### Reversible Processes

Carnot's work on reversible heat engines showed the maximum efficiency is independent of the working substance. Real engines prioritize power over efficiency — thermodynamically reversible processes are infinitely slow and thus useless for practical work.
