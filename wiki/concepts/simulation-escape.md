# Simulation Escape

## The Question

Roman Yampolskiy (University of Louisville) asks: if we are in a simulation, can we escape? And what does "escape" even mean?

## Information as the Goal

The primary motivation for escaping a simulation is **information gain** — accessing more real data about the base reality. Even if nested simulations are infinite, escaping one level up provides:
- More accurate model of the world
- Access to greater computational resources
- Understanding of the simulators' nature

## The Principle of Indifference Problem

The probability of being in a simulation faces a partitioning problem: if simulations can be infinite and of different types (weather, entertainment, science), the principle of indifference (uniform prior over possibilities) gives infinitely small probability of being in base reality. However, the partition of possibility space is itself ambiguous.

## Hacking the Simulation

Yampolskiy maps video game hacking techniques to real-world "magic":

| Video Game Technique | Real-World Analog |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| Cheat codes | Kabbalistic spells, specific phrases/actions |
| Modified physics | Real-world anomalies |
| Extra lives | Reincarnation, consciousness transfer |
| Unlocking abilities | Acquired savant syndrome |

The key insight: if a cheat code works in a game, it implies a designer built it in. Similarly, anomalies in our world (acquired savant syndrome, mystical experiences) might be "features" of the simulation.

## Acquired Savant Syndrome

People who experience brain modification (injury, medication, psychedelics) and gain novel capabilities (playing piano, speaking Chinese, publishing physics papers) without prior training. Yampolskiy suggests this could be "unlocking" pre-existing skills — like paying Tesla to unlock self-driving mode. Alternatively, it could indicate direct access to an avatar with those skills from outside the simulation.

## Nested Simulations

If simulations can be nested infinitely, no escape is truly final. But each successive escape provides more information. The question "is there a real you outside?" has two possibilities:
1. **Avatar model**: a real being plugs into the simulation (like Neo in The Matrix)
2. **Pure software**: the simulated being has no external counterpart (like Mario)

Pure software simulations are easier to create, making option 2 more likely.

## Connection to Consciousness

Psychedelic experiences (DMT, LSD, mushrooms) are sometimes described as accessing "outside" the simulation. Yampolskiy notes the consistency of experiences across individuals (mechanical elves, etc.) as noteworthy but does not endorse strong claims.

## Significance

Simulation escape connects to [[backdoor-science]]: if the universe is a simulation, then "true physics" is the simulator's API, not the simulated laws we observe. This aligns with the thesis that UAPs and time travel exploit bugs or features in the simulation.
